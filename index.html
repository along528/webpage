Insight Health Data Science Fellows Program

The Insight Health Data Science program is an intensive, seven week postdoctoral training fellowship that bridges the gap between academic research and a career in data science. In the first week of the program we come up with a project idea which is to be predictive and functional as a working web application. Once we have developed an idea, we spend the next three weeks building the project independently from scratch. The remainder of the time is spent showing off our projects and learning together about all sorts of data science related topics.

When I was coming up with an idea for my Insight project I knew I wanted to do something that addressed an issue confronting society head on. One particular issue that has concerned me a lot recently is the issue of police violence that has gotten so much attention recently, especially through the sharing of cell phone videos over social media. Furthermore, so many of these incidents seem to be occuring in the black community making it plain to see that police profiling is still pervasive in the US.

I wondered, is it possible to compare police departments that are most susceptible to racial profiling using publicly available data? It turns out that we can using traffic stop data, which is publicly available in some states. My home state of North Carolina recently released an API of nearly every known traffic stop in the state since 2002. That ends up being over 20 million traffic stops! Other states have similar efforts though the quality and availability of the data drops off very quickly. In the end, I was able to also obtain traffic stop data from Connecticut and Illinois as well, resulting in a decent sample from around the US totaling over 50 million traffic stops altogether.

Each of these 50 million traffic stop records has demographic information about the driver and the police department that the officer who performed the stop was affiliated with. Each record also has information about whether or not the driver was searched, and if so, if they found any contraband (weapons, drugs, etc). I used that information to compare the experience of black drivers and white drivers after being stopped by police officers from different departments. The results were striking.

The above chart shows the average search rate (the rate at which someone is searched given that they were stopped) and average hit rate (the rate at which contraband is found given that someone is searched) for a given police department after controlling for the race of the driver. So, a red point corresponds to the average rates for black drivers in a single police department. Similarly, each blue point shows the average rates for white drivers. Comparing the distributions of search rates for black drivers to that of white drivers, we can clearly see that police departments tend to search black drivers more often than white drivers. In fact, the average police department searches black drivers about twice as often as white drivers. But there is also a long high tail, with some police departments searching black drivers even more often than white drivers. For instance, the Chicago Police Department has on average searched black drivers about 6 times as often as white drivers since 2003.

Furthermore, the huge disparity between the search rate for black and white drivers does not seem to be justified by a higher rate of finding contraband in searches, or the hit rate. Indeed, the same chart shows that the distribution of the hit rate for black and white drivers is nearly identical. So, according to the traffic stop data in these three states, black drivers are more likely to be searched than white drivers even though they are not more likely to have contraband. This looks to me like very clear evidence of racial profiling. Thus, by using the disparity between the search rate for black drivers and white drivers I can do what I set out to do: compare departments based on their susceptibility to racial profiling.

It is also fascinating to note that in many places, this difference in the search rate between black and white drivers is fairly persistent. I looked at the ratio of the search rate for black drivers over the search rate for white drivers over 14 years in the three largest police departments in North Carolina.

There what you see is that each of these departments have had a very different level of racial profiling that has been roughly the same over more than a decade. Every year since at least 2002 the Charlotte-Mecklenburg Police Department on average has searched black drivers about twice as often as white drivers. Meanwhile, the Guilford County Sheriff's Office has shown almost no disparity at all and the Wake County Sherrif's Office has fallen somewhere in between. This stationary behavior of the departments supports using the average of the search rates over time. It also tells us that these differences are seemingly hardwired into the police departments and the communities they serve and have been around for a long time.

The ATLAS Experiment

The ATLAS experiment is an enormous collaboration of over 3000 scientists from around 40 countries interested in studying the millions of proton-proton collisions produced by the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) at CERN in Geneva, Switzerland. To do that they built one of the largest and most complex machines in the world. Weighing about as much as the Eifel Tower and standing as tall the Notre Dame Cathedral in Paris, it's like a giant 100 million pixel camera with a shutter speed capable of taking a picture 40 million times a second and with a resolution on the order of microns. It's an impressive machine! The picture above is of a mural painted of the detector outside the ATLAS control room; though the real thing may be even more beautiful.

I've spent the last 6 years working on the ATLAS experiement. Most of that time I was looking at the petabytes of proton-proton collision data collected by the experiement. You might recall the famous discovery of the Higgs boson announced on July 4th, 2012 - Higgsdependence day.

Well, there's me watching the big announcement! (I seem to have made it into an Italian newspaper.)

That was a once in a lifetime discovery which used the same data. But that data is very rich and so can be looked at in myriad ways. Some analyses of the data attempt to discover other new particles besides the Higgs; other analyses use the data simply to better understand the performance and behavior of the massively complicated detector in order to aid in future analyses; still others seek to test the validity of our current best description of the universe, the so-called Standard Model. I have been involved in analyses of all three types.

Searching for the W-prime

In one analysis we were looking for signatures of an exotic particle called the W-prime. The W-prime is only theorized to exist, and if it did then it could be a clue to an entirely unexplored realm beyond the Standard Model. Which would be even more exciting than the Higgs discovery! We compared the data to what we would expect if the only the Standard Model were true (the null hypothesis) and if the W-prime signal were added in on top of that (the alternative hypothesis). You can see a plot of just such a comparison as a function of the most interesting discriminating variable.

The data is in black, while the standard model prediction is shown in the solid stacked histograms and the proposed signals are drawn on top as individual lines. As you can see, the data agrees really well with the Standard Model and has no need for the proposed signal. So we looked and didn't find it. But you should have expected that because if we had you would have heard about it and I would be in the running for the Nobel Prize.

Studying the Muon Trigger system

In another analysis we studied the performance of an online data filtering system called the trigger system. The data comes in so fast from the LHC that there is no way to record it all, so the trigger system uses a combination of fast electronics and computing clusters to make on the fly decisions about which data is the most interesting. In particular, we focused on collisions that produce a very special (and my favorite) particle called the muon, which is like the electron's obese cousin. As you can imagine, this system is incredibly important to understand. If it's not designed properly we could throw out all of our interesting data without getting a chance to look at it! Fortunately, we understand the trigger system very well.

Measuring WWW production

Last but not least, in my thesis analysis we used the data to observe for the first time a process that was predicted by the Standard Model but until the now haven't had a machine that would allow us to be senstive to it. This is a peculiar phenomenon known as WWW or tri-W production. The Standard Model predicts a particle called the W boson which was discovered at CERN way back in 1983 and which won Carlo Rubbia and Simon van der Meer the Nobel Prize. Back then they made the discovery using just a couple of collisions where they observed the production of the W boson on its own. Now, in the modern era of the LHC single W bosons are produced in copious amounts. But W bosons can also be produced as twins or as triplets or in conjunction with other particles, it's just way less likely. And the details of those phenomena, like the rate at which they occur, has important consequences for the Standard Model. Others have looked at many of these production mechanisms, but none had ever before attempted to look at the production of three W bosons simultaneously. where the protons come in from the left and the three W bosons come out on the right. Being the first to look for this type of process presented unique challenges for understanding and interpreting the data, which I was involved deeply in every step of the way. At this moment, the results of the analysis aren't public, but they should be in the next month or two at which point I will go into detail about the analysis. Stay tuned...
